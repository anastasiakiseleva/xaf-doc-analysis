**Taxonomy Sources**

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceted_classification
*Wikipedia overview of faceted classification: items can be tagged with multiple independent attributes (facets) rather than placed in a single hierarchy, enabling flexible multi-dimensional browsing and retrieval.*

https://berkeley.pressbooks.pub/tdo4p/chapter/faceted-classification/
*Open-access textbook chapter covering faceted classification principles: facet analysis, citation order, and practical application in library and information science. Good introductory treatment of how facets are identified and combined.*

Encyclopedia of Database Systems - Faceted Search (pp 1441-1448)
*Academic encyclopedia entry on faceted search as used in databases and e-commerce. Explains how facets enable users to filter large result sets across multiple orthogonal dimensions simultaneously, reducing cognitive load during navigation.*

Guidelines for the Construction, Format, and Management of Monolingual Controlled Vocabularies ANSI/NISO Z39.19-2005 (R2010)
https://perma.cc/ZE7N-E5KY
https://marciazeng.metadataetc.org/Z3919/9Z3919.htm
*The authoritative ANSI/NISO standard for building controlled vocabularies and thesauri. Defines preferred terms, equivalence (USE/UF), hierarchical (BT/NT), and associative (RT) relationships, scope notes, and governance guidelines. Foundation reference for any formal taxonomy.*

https://www.writethedocs.org/guide/writing/mindshare/#build-a-taxonomy
*Write the Docs practitioner guide on building a documentation taxonomy. Covers how to map user and community language to documentation concepts, and how shared vocabulary reduces friction between writers and readers.*

https://www.w3.org/TR/skos-primer/
*W3C primer for the Simple Knowledge Organization System (SKOS), an RDF vocabulary for expressing thesauri, classification schemes, and controlled vocabularies as linked data. Covers concepts, concept schemes, broader/narrower/related relationships, and labels — the basis for interoperable semantic vocabularies.*

https://community.rws.com/product-groups/tridion/b/blog/posts/leveraging-the-power-of-knowledge-graphs-in-technical-documentation
*RWS (major content management and translation platform) blog post on applying knowledge graphs to technical documentation. Argues that graph-based structure improves content discoverability, reuse, and navigation compared to flat or tree-structured content models.*

https://enterprise-knowledge.com/why-a-knowledge-graph-is-the-best-way-to-upgrade-your-taxonomy/
*Enterprise Knowledge consulting firm blog post on combining knowledge graphs with traditional taxonomies. Argues that graph-based relationships capture semantics that hierarchical taxonomies cannot, and that upgrading a taxonomy to a knowledge graph makes it more useful for search, recommendation, and AI applications.*

https://arxiv.org/html/2408.04948v1
*HybridRAG (Sarmah et al., BlackRock/NVIDIA, 2024): proposes combining vector-based RAG (VectorRAG) and knowledge graph-based RAG (GraphRAG) for information extraction from financial documents. HybridRAG outperforms both individual approaches on faithfulness, answer relevancy, and context recall — directly relevant to mixing semantic similarity search with structured graph retrieval in this project.*

Exploiting a Controlled Vocabulary to Improve Collection Selection and Retrieval Effectiveness
*Information retrieval research paper demonstrating that controlled vocabulary indexing improves both recall and precision when selecting relevant document collections. Provides empirical grounding for the value of consistent concept labeling across a corpus.*

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8830400/
*DeMars & Perruso (2022), "MeSH and text-word search strategies: precision, recall, and their implications for library instruction." Empirical study comparing MeSH (controlled vocabulary) vs. free-text keyword search in PubMed: MeSH yielded 75% recall and 47.7% precision vs. 54% recall and 34.4% precision for text-words. Conclusion: controlled vocabulary outperforms keyword search, but a combination of both achieves the most comprehensive coverage. Directly supports the rationale for maintaining a formal XAF taxonomy rather than relying on free-text matching alone.*
